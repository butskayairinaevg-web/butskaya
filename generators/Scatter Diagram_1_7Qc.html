Scatter Diagram (Correlation Chart / Scatter Plot / Scatter Graph)

A scatter diagram is a graphical tool that plots pairs of numerical data points to reveal the relationship between two variables. One variable appears on the horizontal X-axis (independent variable), and the other on the vertical Y-axis (dependent variable). Also known as a scatter plot, scatter chart, correlation chart, or scatter graph, it visually shows patterns like lines or curves when variables correlate strongly.

Key Uses

Scatter diagrams help identify positive, negative, or no correlation between variables. They are one of the 7 QC Tools used in quality management and support Six Sigma and other improvement methodologies by visualizing process relationships without requiring complex calculations.

Correlation Patterns

  • Positive correlation: Points trend from lower-left to upper-right; one variable increases as the other increases.
  • Negative correlation: Points trend from upper-left to lower-right; one variable increases while the other decreases.
  • No correlation: Points appear randomly; no clear relationship exists.

Unlike a Fishbone Diagram, which categorizes causes and effects qualitatively, a scatter diagram uses numerical data to quantify and visualize actual relationships. This supports better decision-making in manufacturing, quality control, analytics, and statistics.

2. Why Scatter Diagrams Are Used

Scatter graphs are powerful because they help you:

  1. Identify correlation (positive, negative, or none).
  2. Verify cause-and-effect relationships.
  3. Understand process behaviour.
  4. Support decision-making in problem solving (7 QC Tools).
  5. Analyse trends for process improvement (SPC, CAPA, FMEA).

3. When to Use a Scatter Diagram

Use a scatter diagram when:

  • You want to check if two variables are related (e.g., temperature vs. defect rate).
  • You suspect one factor is influencing another.
  • You want to validate hypotheses in Root Cause Analysis (RCA).
  • You are using DMAIC / PDCA improvement cycles.

4. Components of a Scatter Diagram

A scatter diagram includes:

  • Horizontal axis (X) – independent variable
  • Vertical axis (Y) – dependent variable
  • Data points – plotted pairs (X, Y)
  • Trend direction – slope indicating correlation
  • Optional regression line – line of best fit

5. Types of Correlation in Scatter Diagrams

A. Positive Correlation

As X increases, Y also increases.
Example: Input pressure increases → Flow rate increases.
Pattern: Upward slope.

B. Negative Correlation

As X increases, Y decreases.
Example: Machine idle time increases → Production output decreases.
Pattern: Downward slope.

C. No Correlation

Changes in X do not affect Y.
Example: Operator age vs. number of defects.
Pattern: Randomly scattered points.

6. Strength of Correlation

Correlation is not only about direction but also about strength:

  • Strong correlation → Points are close to a straight line.
  • Weak correlation → Points are spread out.
  • Zero correlation → No visible pattern.

7. How to Create a Scatter Diagram

Follow these steps:

  1. Collect paired data for X and Y. (Example: Temperature vs. Viscosity — 10 samples).
  2. Organise data in two columns: Column 1 → X values, Column 2 → Y values.
  3. Draw axes and label the variables.
  4. Plot the data points for each (X, Y) pair.
  5. Observe the pattern to determine correlation.
  6. Optional: Add a trend line using Excel.

8. Real-World Examples of Scatter Diagram Applications

Manufacturing

Temperature vs. Defect rate; Torque vs. Leakage; Raw material hardness vs. Wear rate; Pressure vs. Flow variation.

Quality Control / SPC

Process time vs. Rejection; Diameter vs. Weight; Machine speed vs. Surface finish.

Service Industry

Waiting time vs. Customer satisfaction; Marketing spend vs. Sales volume.

10. Advantages of Scatter Diagram

  • Simple and easy to use.
  • Shows real data behaviour.
  • Visual tool for correlation analysis.
  • Validates cause-and-effect assumptions.
  • Supports quality improvement initiatives.

11. Limitations

  • Shows correlation, not causation.
  • Requires sufficient data.
  • Does not show the strength numerically unless combined with correlation coefficient (r).
  • Difficult to interpret when data points overlap heavily.

12. Important Note & Purpose

Important: Correlation does not always mean causation. Use scatter diagrams along with FMEA, 5 Why, and Process Study to confirm the root cause.

SCATTER GRAPH – PURPOSE

A Scatter Graph is used to understand how two numerical variables are related. Its purposes include:

  • To analyze the strength and relationship between two variables.
  • To provide data that confirms a hypothesis regarding whether two variables are related.
  • For better process management in variable analysis.
  • To establish a relationship between two sets of numerical data.
  • To track trends and patterns of different process measures.
  • To investigate the relationship between one variable and another.
  • To validate assumptions during the root cause analysis process.
  • To support data-driven decision-making in quality and process improvement.

Correlation Categories (5 Types)

  1. Strong or High Positive Correlation
  2. Strong or High Negative Correlation
  3. Weak or Low Positive Correlation
  4. Weak or Low Negative Correlation
  5. Weakest or No Correlation

Scatter Diagram vs. Fishbone Diagram

Aspect               | Scatter Diagram               | Fishbone Diagram
---------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------
Purpose              | Shows relationship strength   | Identifies possible causes
Shows correlation?   | Yes                           | No
Data required        | Quantitative (paired values)  | Qualitative brainstorming
Output               | Pattern indicating correlation| Structured list of causes
