Fishbone Diagram (Ishikawa / Cause & Effect)

Full content in one scroll, with sidebar links and light/dark mode.

1. Introduction

A Fishbone Diagram, also called the Ishikawa Diagram or Cause-and-Effect Diagram, is a visual problem-solving tool used to identify and analyze all possible causes of a specific problem. It is widely applied in Quality Control (QC), Quality Assurance (QA), Lean, Six Sigma, manufacturing, services, and Root Cause Analysis (RCA).

The diagram helps teams understand why a problem occurs by displaying causes in a structured “fishbone” layout radiating from a central spine.

2. Why it is called a Fishbone Diagram

  • Head → represents the effect or problem.
  • Spine → the main line connecting causes to the effect.
  • Main bones → categories of causes (Man, Machine, Method, etc.).
  • Small bones → sub‑causes under each category.

3. Names and Origin

  • Ishikawa Diagram → named after Professor Kaoru Ishikawa (Japan, mid‑20th century).
  • Cause & Effect Diagram → shows how causes combine to create an effect.
  • Fishbone / Herringbone Diagram → its shape looks like a fish skeleton.

4. Purpose & Use

  • Identify all possible causes of a problem, not just obvious ones.
  • Organize causes in a structured, visual format for discussion.
  • Show relationships between effect and multiple cause categories.
  • Support systematic root cause analysis and avoid symptom‑based quick fixes.
  • Strengthen RCA in Lean, Six Sigma, Kaizen and CAPA activities.

Most useful when:

  • Problems are complex or cross‑functional.
  • Multiple possible causes exist.
  • Issues are recurring and root cause is not clear.
  • Structured brainstorming and visual clarity are required.

5. Common Cause Categories (6M Method)

The 6M model is common in manufacturing environments.

1. Man (People)

  • Skill level and competency.
  • Training and awareness.
  • Communication gaps and misunderstanding.
  • Fatigue, stress, motivation.
  • Human error.

2. Machine (Equipment)

  • Wear and tear of machines or tools.
  • Lack of preventive maintenance.
  • Calibration issues.
  • Tooling problems and breakdowns.
  • Capability or capacity limits.

3. Method (Process)

  • Incorrect or unclear procedures.
  • Poor work instructions or SOPs.
  • Non‑standardized processes.
  • Wrong sequence of operations.
  • Inefficient techniques or rework loops.

4. Material (Raw Material)

  • Poor material quality.
  • Specification mismatch.
  • Impurity or contamination.
  • Availability or stock issues.
  • Handling and storage problems.

5. Measurement (Inspection)

  • Wrong measuring tool.
  • Calibration errors.
  • Incorrect measurement method.
  • Data entry or transcription errors.
  • Gauge variation and repeatability issues.

6. Mother Nature / Environment

  • Temperature and humidity.
  • Lighting and visibility.
  • Cleanliness of area.
  • Noise and vibration.
  • Other environmental conditions affecting the process.

6. Cause Categories for Service Industry

Service and office processes often use people‑ and process‑focused categories instead of 6M.

4P Model

  • People
  • Process
  • Policies
  • Place

8P Model

  • Product
  • Price
  • Promotion
  • Place
  • People
  • Process
  • Physical Evidence
  • Performance

7. How to Use a Fishbone Diagram (Step‑by‑Step)

Step 1: Define the Problem Clearly

  • Write a clear problem statement, for example “High customer complaints for late delivery”.

Step 2: Draw the Spine and Head

  • Draw a horizontal arrow (spine) and write the problem inside a box at the head on the right side.

Step 3: Add Major Cause Categories

  • Use 6M, 4P, 8P, or custom categories as main bones.

Step 4: Brainstorm Causes

  • Ask the team “What could cause this problem?”.
  • List causes under appropriate categories.

Step 5: Add Sub‑Causes Using 5 Whys

  • For each cause, keep asking “Why?” until deeper root causes appear.

Step 6: Analyze and Identify Key Causes

  • Look for causes that appear repeatedly or seem strongly linked to the effect.

Step 7: Implement Corrective Actions

  • Define corrective and preventive actions to address root causes permanently.

When to use an Ishikawa Diagram?

  • Problem‑solving for quality issues.
  • To analyze interactions among multiple factors.
  • To prepare CAPA and audit responses (ISO / IATF).
  • In combination with 5 Whys, Pareto charts, check sheets, etc.

8. Steps for making “Cause and Effect Diagram” (Compact)

Step‑1: Identify and define the problem/effect using the 5W2H approach.

Step‑2: Write the problem in the problem box and draw the spine.

Step‑3: Identify 4M/6M categories (primary causes) and draw main bones.

Step‑4: Identify probable secondary causes using brainstorming and check sheets.

Step‑5: Add tertiary causes and analyze the diagram using 5 Whys.

9. Benefits of Fishbone Diagram

  • Simple and beginner‑friendly.
  • Encourages team brainstorming and participation.
  • Excellent for visualizing complex, multi‑cause problems.
  • Focuses attention on root causes instead of symptoms.
  • Applicable in all industries and functions.
  • Works well with 5 Whys, Pareto Chart and other RCA tools.

10. Limitations

  • Quality of analysis depends on team knowledge and data.
  • May miss hidden or systemic causes.
  • Does not automatically confirm the true root cause.
  • Diagram can become cluttered if too many causes are added.

11. Tips for Effective Use

  • Define the problem clearly and specifically.
  • Involve cross‑functional team members who know the process.
  • Use 5 Whys for deeper analysis of key branches.
  • Validate suspected causes with real data and observations.
  • Prioritize causes using Pareto analysis where possible.
  • Keep the layout clean and readable for reviews and audits.
