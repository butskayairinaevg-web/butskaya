Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (FMEA) – Meaning

Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (FMEA) is a systematic and structured method used to identify potential failure modes in a product, process, or system, assess their effects, and prioritize actions to reduce the associated risks.

The main objectives of FMEA are:

  • To identify possible failure modes before they occur.
  • To understand the potential effects of these failures on the customer, safety, function, and performance.
  • To identify the causes of these failures and implement preventive or corrective actions.
  • To improve the reliability, safety, and robustness of products and processes.

In simple terms, FMEA helps a team anticipate problems before they happen and define actions to prevent or control those problems, rather than reacting after failures occur.

FMEA is one of the five Core Tools of IATF 16949:2016:

  • Advanced Product Quality Planning (APQP)
  • Production Part Approval Process (PPAP)
  • Failure Mode and Effect Analysis (FMEA)
  • Measurement System Analysis (MSA)
  • Statistical Process Control (SPC)

Types of FMEA

There are two main types of FMEA commonly used in industry:

1. Design FMEA (DFMEA)

  • Focuses on failures related to the design of the product.
  • Evaluates potential issues with material selection, dimensions, tolerances, strength, performance, and safety.
  • Is performed during the product design and development phase, before releasing drawings for production.
  • Typical concerns include:
    • Insufficient strength leading to fatigue or breakage.
    • Incorrect material choice causing corrosion or wear.
    • Dimensional or tolerance issues causing misfit or misalignment.
  • The objective is to make the design robust, safe, and capable of meeting customer and regulatory requirements.

2. Process FMEA (PFMEA)

  • Focuses on failures related to the manufacturing or assembly process.
  • Evaluates potential issues in process steps, machines, methods, material handling, inspection, and human activities.
  • Is performed during process planning and industrialization, before mass production starts.
  • Typical concerns include:
    • Incorrect torque leading to loose fasteners.
    • Incorrect welding parameters causing weak welds.
    • Mix-up of parts due to poor identification or process control.
  • The objective is to ensure that the process is capable, controlled, and not likely to produce defects.

Summary:
DFMEA asks: “What can go wrong in the design?”
PFMEA asks: “What can go wrong in the process?”

When Should FMEA Be Started?

👉 FMEA should always be started as early as possible in the design or process development phase, when changes are easier and less expensive to implement.

For Design FMEA (DFMEA)

  • Start after the product concept has been defined but before design is frozen.
  • Perform DFMEA while requirements, specifications, and customer expectations are being translated into design features.
  • The goal is to identify potential design weaknesses before releasing drawings for tooling and production.

For Process FMEA (PFMEA)

  • Start during process design and planning, when process flow, layout, equipment, and control plans are being developed.
  • PFMEA should be completed before the start of mass production.
  • The goal is to identify and mitigate process risks before they can affect production and customers.

Early FMEA means fewer changes during production, lower cost, fewer customer issues, and higher confidence in the design and process.

AIAG & VDA FMEA – 7 Step Approach

The AIAG & VDA FMEA Handbook (1st Edition 2019) introduced a new 7-step approach that is more structured and prevention-focused compared to the earlier 5-step method.

Step 1 – Planning and Preparation

  • Define the scope, boundaries, and objectives of the FMEA.
  • Collect relevant customer requirements, technical specifications, and historical data.
  • Form a cross-functional team (Design, Process, Quality, Production, Supplier, etc.).

Step 2 – Structure Analysis

Break down the system, design, or process into a structured hierarchy:

  • Process Item – e.g. an assembly line or overall process.
  • Process Step – e.g. machining, pressing, welding, assembly operations.
  • Process Work Element – e.g. operator actions, machine functions, material movements.

Step 3 – Functional Analysis

Define the functions and requirements at each level:

  • Functions of the process item.
  • Functions of each process step and related product characteristics.
  • Functions of each work element and related process characteristics.

Step 4 – Failure Analysis

Identify how each function can fail:

  • Failure modes – How can the function fail?
  • Effects of failure – What happens if the failure occurs (impact on customer, safety, or system)?
  • Causes of failure – Why would the failure occur (root causes)?

Step 5 – Risk Analysis

  • Evaluate the Severity (S), Occurrence (O), and Detection (D) ratings.
  • Instead of using RPN (S×O×D), the new method uses Action Priority (AP).
  • AP tables classify combinations of S, O, and D into High, Medium, or Low action priority.
  • High Action Priority items require immediate and effective actions.

Step 6 – Optimization

  • Define and implement actions to reduce risk (design changes, process improvements, error-proofing, controls, training, etc.).
  • Update the FMEA with revised S, O, and D ratings after actions are implemented.
  • Ensure that residual risk is acceptable and documented.

Step 7 – Documentation

  • Record all relevant information, decisions, and actions in the FMEA document.
  • Maintain transparency for audits, lessons learned, and future projects.

AIAG & VDA FMEA | 1st Edition 2019

Historically, AIAG (Automotive Industry Action Group – USA) and VDA (German Association of the Automotive Industry) had separate FMEA manuals with different rating tables, formats, and expectations.

In 2019, AIAG and VDA jointly released the AIAG & VDA FMEA Handbook (1st Edition) to provide a harmonized, global FMEA approach.

Key features of the AIAG & VDA FMEA Handbook:

  • Replaces the old AIAG 4th Edition FMEA and VDA Volume 4 FMEA manuals.
  • Introduces a 7-step structured methodology for FMEA.
  • Replaces the RPN concept with Action Priority (AP) for better decision making.
  • Introduces FMEA-MSR (Monitoring and System Response) for system-level and in-use risk evaluation.
  • Provides a common, globally recognized framework for Design FMEA, Process FMEA, and System FMEA.

The main focus of this handbook is to strengthen prevention, improve transparency in risk assessment, and align expectations between OEMs and suppliers worldwide.

Why Alignment Between AIAG & VDA Was Needed

1. Different Approaches for the Same Supplier

Many automotive suppliers work with both North American (AIAG-based) and German (VDA-based) OEMs. Before alignment, they had to maintain different FMEA formats and rating methods for different customers, which increased complexity and workload.

2. Confusing Rating and Risk Criteria

Severity, Occurrence, and Detection rating tables were different between AIAG and VDA. The same failure could receive different ratings and RPNs, leading to confusion when comparing risk levels across customers or regions.

3. Extra Work and Documentation Overlap

Suppliers sometimes had to prepare two different FMEA versions for the same part or process – one for AIAG-style expectations and one for VDA-style expectations. This duplicated effort and made audits and maintenance more difficult.

4. Need for One Global Standard

The automotive industry needed a single, harmonized FMEA standard that would be accepted globally. This would:

  • Simplify the FMEA process for suppliers.
  • Provide clear and common expectations for OEMs.
  • Make training, auditing, and continuous improvement more efficient.

The AIAG & VDA FMEA Handbook is the result of this alignment and provides one common FMEA “language” for the global automotive supply chain.

Why AIAG & VDA FMEA? | Key Benefits

Modern vehicles, machines, and systems are highly complex. With increasing complexity, the potential for failure also increases. FMEA helps organizations systematically identify and reduce these risks before they affect the customer.

Main Benefits of FMEA

  • Early Risk Identification: Risks are detected and evaluated during design and process development.
  • Improved Reliability: Products and processes become more stable and reliable over time.
  • Prevention Culture: Shifts the organization from a reactive “fire-fighting” approach to a preventive mindset.
  • Reduced Customer Issues: Complaints, rejections, warranty claims, and field failures are reduced.
  • Cost Savings: Less rework, scrap, recalls, and legal exposure due to failures.
  • Better Communication: Cross-functional teams use a common document to discuss and manage risk.

FMEA is not just a form to fill in; it is a thinking process that strengthens the organization’s approach to risk and quality.

7 Types of Failure Modes in FMEA

In AIAG & VDA FMEA, failure analysis pays special attention to typical ways in which a function can deviate from the intended performance. These are often described through seven types of functional behaviour.

1. Loss of Function

  • The function does not occur at all.
  • Example (Ball Pen): The pen does not write; no ink flows to the paper.

2. Degradation of Function

  • The function is present but deteriorates over time or performance becomes weaker.
  • Example: The pen writes well initially, but later the ink becomes faint or inconsistent.

3. Intermittent Function

  • The function starts and stops irregularly, with no consistent pattern.
  • Example: While writing, some letters appear correctly and others are missing because ink flow is intermittent.

4. Partial Function

  • The function is performed, but not to the required level.
  • Example: The pen writes, but the ink coverage is poor and the writing looks patchy.

5. Exceeding Function

  • The function output exceeds what is required.
  • Example: The pen releases too much ink, causing smearing or spreading on the paper.

6. Delayed Function

  • The function occurs but not at the required time; it is delayed.
  • Example: After pressing or clicking the pen, the ink flow starts only after some delay.

7. Unintended Function

  • The system produces an unexpected or opposite outcome compared to what is intended.
  • Example (Steering System): The driver turns the steering wheel to the left, but the vehicle responds incorrectly and moves to the right.

Understanding these failure behaviours helps the FMEA team to systematically analyze how functions can deviate and what risks must be addressed through design and process controls.

Design FMEA (DFMEA) – AIAG & VDA Format

DFMEA evaluates potential failure modes in the product design before the product is released for production. The purpose is to identify design weaknesses early and improve functional robustness, safety, and performance.

Action Priority (AP) Table – AIAG & VDA FMEA 2019

The AIAG & VDA handbook replaces the RPN concept with Action Priority (AP), which guides the team on whether action is required to reduce risk. AP is determined based on the Severity (S), Occurrence (O), and Detection (D) rating combination.

AP Levels:

  • H – High Priority: Action is required.
  • M – Medium Priority: Action should be considered.
  • L – Low Priority: Action is optional.
Severity (S) Occurrence (O) Detection (D) Action Priority (AP)
9–10 Any value (1–10) Any value (1–10) High (H) – Action required
7–8 ≥ 5 ≥ 5 High (H)
5–6 ≥ 7 ≥ 7 High (H)
7–8 3–4 ≥ 6 Medium (M)
5–6 4–6 ≥ 5 Medium (M)
3–4 ≥ 6 ≥ 6 Medium (M)
1–4 1–3 1–3 Low (L)
5–6 1–3 1–3 Low (L)
7–8 1–2 2–3 Low (L)

Note: Action Priority does not replace engineering judgment. It helps the team determine which S-O-D combinations need urgent action to reduce risk.

Control Plan – Product & Process (AIAG Format)

A Control Plan defines how product and process characteristics will be controlled to ensure consistent quality during production. It is directly connected to FMEA and is one of the mandatory APQP deliverables.
