MSA | Measurement System Analysis ▾

Definition: Measurement System Analysis (MSA) is a statistical method used to evaluate the accuracy, precision, and reliability of a measurement system. It determines how much variation in measurement data comes from the measurement process itself, not from the product.

Purpose: The main goal of MSA is to check whether the measurement system is capable of producing accurate, consistent, and reliable results.

Key Focus Areas:

  • Equipment Variation (EV): Variation caused by the measuring instrument itself.
  • Appraiser/Personnel Variation (AV): Variation caused by the person who takes measurement.

How it works:

  • The measurement system is tested to calculate statistical properties such as bias, repeatability, and reproducibility.
  • These results are compared with accepted standards to check suitability for use.

Importance in Quality: MSA is one of the 5 Core Tools of Quality required by IATF 16949:2016:

  • APQP – Advanced Product Quality Planning
  • PPAP – Production Part Approval Process
  • FMEA – Failure Mode and Effects Analysis
  • MSA – Measurement System Analysis
  • SPC – Statistical Process Control

MSA – Definition & Components ▾

Measurement System Analysis (MSA) evaluates accuracy, precision, and reliability of measurement systems.

Components of a Measurement System:

  • Operations: Steps followed during measurement.
  • Procedures and Methods: Standard techniques and instructions used.
  • Personnel: The people (appraisers/inspectors) performing measurement.
  • Environment: Surroundings that may affect measurement (temperature, humidity, noise).
  • Equipment / Gage / Instrument: The physical tool or device used.
  • Software: Any program or digital tool used to record, calculate, or analyse data.
  • Assumptions: Conditions or expectations considered during the measurement process.

Types of MSA ▾

1. Variable MSA Study

Definition: Variable MSA is used when the measurement data is quantitative (numerical/continuous values). Examples: length, width, diameter, weight, thickness, pressure, temperature.

Key Tool: Gage R&R (Repeatability & Reproducibility)

  • Repeatability: Variation when the same operator measures the same part using the same instrument multiple times.
  • Reproducibility: Variation when different operators measure the same part using the same instrument.

Example: Measuring the diameter of a shaft with a Vernier Caliper (values like 20.01 mm).

2. Attribute MSA Study

Definition: Attribute MSA is used when the measurement data is qualitative (categorical/discrete values). Examples: Go/No-Go, Pass/Fail, OK/Not OK.

Key Tool: Attribute Agreement Analysis (AAA)

  • Checks the consistency of decision-making between operators.
  • Looks at agreement within the same inspector (repeatability) and between different inspectors (reproducibility).

Example: Checking whether a painted part is OK or NOT OK by visual inspection.

Variable MSA → deals with numbers; Attribute MSA → deals with categories.

MSA Applications ▾

  • To assess new measuring equipment/process for acceptance or improvement.
  • Compare one measuring device with another to see which is more accurate/reliable.
  • Before & after repair check to confirm improvement.
  • Verify measuring instruments after calibration.
  • Validate measurement systems for PPAP submissions.
In short: MSA is used to check, compare, and improve measurement systems.

Why MSA is Required? ▾

  • For Correct Decisions: Decisions in manufacturing depend on measurement data quality. Wrong data → wrong decisions.
  • To Reduce Measurement Errors: MSA helps find and reduce errors in the measurement system.
  • To Prevent Wrong Judgments: Without MSA operators may accept bad parts or reject good parts.
  • To Assure Customer Quality: MSA ensures accurate inspection and prevents defective parts reaching customers.
Reliable data → correct decisions, fewer mistakes, improved customer satisfaction.

Measurement Issues ▾

Three fundamental issues must be addressed:

  1. Adequate discrimination: The system should detect small differences (e.g., 10.01 mm vs 10.05 mm).
  2. Statistical stability over time: Same part measured over time should show stable results.
  3. Consistency across range: Accuracy must hold across the full measurement range.

Measurement System Errors ▾

Five main types of errors:

  • Bias: Average measurement differs from true value (e.g., 1.05 kg vs 1.00 kg).
  • Stability: Measurement changes over time.
  • Linearity: Error varies across measurement range.
  • Repeatability: Same operator gives different results on repeated measures.
  • Reproducibility: Different operators get different results.
Location errors: Bias, Stability, Linearity. Width errors: Repeatability, Reproducibility.

Measurement System Variation ▾

Sources of variation:

  • Instrument defects or calibration issues
  • Operator differences in usage
  • Method/procedure inconsistencies
  • Environmental factors (temperature, humidity, vibration)
  • Part characteristics (surface, shape, dirt)
Variation arises from people, instruments, methods, environment, and parts.

GRR Acceptance Criteria ▾

Gage R&R indicates % variation from measurement system. Acceptance levels:

%GRRInterpretationAction
≤ 10%Acceptable — Very good systemUse without concern
10% – 30%Marginal — Use with cautionImprovement recommended
≥ 30%Not acceptableMust improve/change system
Good ≤ 10% • Okay but needs improvement 10–30% • Bad ≥ 30%

NDC — Number of Distinct Categories ▾

NDC shows how many unique groups the measurement system can differentiate.

  • NDC < 5: Poor system
  • NDC 5–10: Moderate system
  • NDC > 10: Excellent system

Attribute MSA Metrics ▾

  • Overall Accuracy: % of correct decisions.
  • Repeatability: Operator consistent with self.
  • Reproducibility: Different operators agree.
  • Bias (Consumer vs Producer risk): Consumer’s Risk: Accepting defective parts. Producer’s Risk: Rejecting good parts.

Typical MSA Study Requirements ▾

  • 3 Operators
  • 10 Parts
  • 2–3 Trials
  • Parts covering full tolerance range
  • Stable environment
  • No communication between appraisers during study

Common Causes of Poor MSA ▾

  • Worn-out instruments
  • Poor calibration
  • Operator lacks training
  • Bad measurement method
  • Unclear inspection criteria
  • Temperature/humidity variation
  • Poor part preparation
  • Human judgment errors (esp. for Attribute MSA)

How to Improve a Poor Measurement System ▾

  • Improve calibration
  • Use fixtures to reduce operator error
  • Provide training
  • Improve work instructions
  • Reduce environment influence
  • Use digital instruments
  • Simplify method
  • Use error-proofing (poka-yoke)

Complete Summary ▾

MSA ensures measurement reliability. It includes both variable & attribute studies. Gage R&R measures repeatability & reproducibility. Good system: %GRR ≤ 10%, NDC > 10. Attribute MSA checks decision consistency. Errors: Bias, stability, linearity, repeatability, reproducibility. Accurate measurement = correct decision, less rejection, high customer satisfaction.
