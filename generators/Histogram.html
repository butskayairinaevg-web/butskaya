1. What is a Histogram?

A histogram is a graphical representation of data that shows how frequently values occur within defined ranges (called bins or intervals).

It is one of the 7 QC Tools and is widely used in manufacturing to understand variation and distribution.

In simple terms:
A histogram shows the shape of data and helps determine whether the process behaves normally or abnormally.

2. Purpose and Importance of a Histogram

A histogram helps you answer important questions such as:

  • Is the process centered or shifted?
  • Is the variation normal or excessive?
  • Does the data follow a known distribution?
  • Are special causes affecting the process?
  • Is the process stable and capable?

Key uses:

  • Identifying patterns of variation
  • Understanding spread and central tendency
  • Supporting process improvements
  • Root-cause analysis of defects
  • Monitoring product dimensions, weights, etc.

3. Components of a Histogram

  1. Bins / Class Intervals – Data ranges (e.g., 9.80–9.85 mm)
  2. Frequency – Number of data points in each bin
  3. Bars – Height represents frequency
  4. X-axis – Measurement values
  5. Y-axis – Frequency count
Important: Bars in a histogram touch each other, because the data is continuous.

4. Steps to Construct a Histogram (Step-by-Step)

Step 1: Collect Data
Example: 50 readings of part diameter.

Step 2: Find Minimum and Maximum Values
Minimum = 9.85 mm
Maximum = 10.25 mm

Step 3: Calculate Range
Range = Maximum − Minimum = 10.25 − 9.85 = 0.40 mm

Step 4: Decide the Number of Bins
Typical: 5–12 bins
Bins = 1 + 3.3 log(n)

Step 5: Calculate Bin Width
Bin Width = Range / Number of bins

Step 6: Create Class Intervals

  • 9.85–9.90
  • 9.90–9.95
  • 9.95–10.00
  • 10.00–10.05
  • 10.05–10.10
  • 10.10–10.15
  • 10.15–10.20
  • 10.20–10.25

Step 7: Count Frequencies for Each Interval

  • 9.85–9.90 → 3
  • 9.90–9.95 → 6
  • 9.95–10.00 → 10
  • 10.00–10.05 → 12
  • 10.05–10.10 → 9
  • 10.10–10.15 → 6
  • 10.15–10.20 → 3
  • 10.20–10.25 → 1

Step 8: Draw the Histogram – Plot bars with heights equal to the frequencies.

5. Types of Histogram Shapes and Interpretation

5.1 Normal Distribution (Bell-Shaped)

  • Symmetrical around the mean
  • Variation due to common causes
  • Indicates a stable and predictable process

5.2 Skewed Distribution

  • Data leans to one side
  • Process may be shifting
  • Possible reasons: tool wear, incorrect setup, measurement bias

5.3 Bimodal Distribution

  • Indicates two different data sources
  • Two machines / operators / mixed batches

5.4 Wide or Flat Distribution

  • High variation
  • Process not under control

5.5 Truncated or Cut-Off Distribution

  • Missing data at one end
  • Gauge filtering or inspection bias

6. Applications of Histograms in Quality Control

  • Process performance analysis
  • Capability studies (Cp, Cpk)
  • Machine stability and wear detection
  • Incoming and final inspection
  • Supplier quality monitoring
  • Rejection and defect pattern analysis
  • SPC (Statistical Process Control)
