10.HOW TO BUILD A PARETO CHART (STEP-BY-STEP)▼
Step 1: Collect and Categorize Data

Begin with a clean and complete dataset that represents the problems, defects, or outcomes you want to analyse.

Examples in manufacturing include:

  • Defect types
  • Machine downtime reasons
  • Supplier-wise rejection categories
  • Assembly errors
  • Customer complaints

Group each observation into consistent, meaningful categories such as:

  • Scratched part
  • Missing label
  • Incorrect dimension
  • Loose fitting
Best Practice:

Avoid creating too many categories. Over-categorizing spreads your data thin and makes patterns harder to identify.

Step 2: Sort Categories

Count how often each category occurs. If you are measuring cost or impact instead of frequency, compute the total value for each category.

Then sort the categories in descending order based on:

  • Highest frequency
  • Highest cost
  • Highest impact

The most significant issues should always appear at the top of the list. This ordering is essential because the Pareto Chart arranges bars from largest to smallest, enabling clear prioritization.

Step 3: Calculate Percentages and Cumulative Percentages

1. Percentage of Total

( Category Value / Total Value ) × 100

Example: If a defect occurs 20 times out of 100 total defects:
(20 / 100) × 100 = 20%

2. Cumulative Percentage

This is the running total of percentages as you move down the sorted list.

  • First category: 40% → cumulative = 40%
  • Second category: 25% → cumulative = 65%
  • Third category: 15% → cumulative = 80%

The final cumulative percentage will always reach 100%.

Step 4: Build the Chart (Bars + Line)

A Pareto Chart is a combination chart:

Bars (Vertical Bar Graph)
  • Represent the frequency or cost of each category
  • Arranged from tallest to shortest
Line (Cumulative Percentage Line)
  • Plotted above the bars
  • Shows how each category contributes to the total
  • Helps identify the "vital few" issues

When the line is steep, the categories have a high cumulative impact. When it flattens, the remaining categories have minimal influence.

Step 5: Interpret the Chart and Prioritize Actions
  • The tallest bars → your biggest problems
  • The point where the cumulative line begins to flatten → diminishing returns
  • The 80% zone → the most significant causes contributing to most of your issues
Key Insight:

Often, fixing just 20–30% of the categories will eliminate 70–80% of the total problems. This is the foundation of the Pareto Principle (80/20 rule).

11.USE CASES BEYOND MANUFACTURING▼

Pareto Charts originated in quality engineering, but the logic applies anywhere you need to identify which issues create the greatest impact.

Software Bugs – Effects of Using Pareto Charts in System Testing
  • Helps identify major defect categories by grouping hundreds of logged issues into logical clusters (e.g., authentication, API, UI).
  • Enables teams to rank problem types by frequency or impact.
  • Reveals that a small number of categories cause most defects.
  • Allows teams to focus on fixing high-impact issues first.
  • Reduces rework and saves time.
  • Improves system stability and reliability.
  • Supports data-driven decision-making.
Customer Complaints – Effects of Pareto Charts in Support Centers
  • Groups diverse complaints into categories.
  • Reveals that 2–3 categories often cause 80% of dissatisfaction.
  • Enables leaders to prioritize fixes.
  • Boosts customer experience.
  • Improves retention rates.
  • Reduces overall complaint volume.
Sales Analysis – Effects of Pareto Analysis
  • Uncovers that few SKUs drive most revenue.
  • Identifies key customers generating majority of sales.
  • Highlights top-performing sales channels.
  • Supports inventory optimization.
Maintenance & Field Service – Effects of Pareto Charts
  • Pinpoints chronic failures.
  • Identifies frequent breakdowns.
  • Drives preventive maintenance.
  • Lowers overall maintenance costs.
Universal Purpose
  • A Pareto Chart highlights the few categories that contribute most to a problem or outcome.
  • This principle applies across many domains.
Role with Root Cause Analysis
  • A Pareto Chart tells which problems to investigate first.
  • Teams then apply 5 Whys, Fishbone, or FMEA.
12.HOW TO INTERPRET THE CUMULATIVE LINE▼
Steep Curve
  • High-impact categories
  • Fix first
Flattening Curve
  • Low frequency
  • Low impact
  • Fix only if required for safety, compliance, or risk
80/20 Identification

The point where the cumulative line crosses 70–80% marks your vital few.

What the Flattening Means
  • Each additional category contributes only a small fraction of the problem.
  • Issues appearing once or twice a month usually fall in this zone.
  • Fixing these items will not significantly shift total performance metrics.

14.ADVANTAGES AND LIMITATIONS▼
Advantages
  1. Identifies major contributors quickly
  2. Simplifies communication
  3. Supports data-driven decisions
  4. Drives focused improvement
Limitations
  1. Rare but severe problems may be overlooked
  2. Requires clean categorization
  3. Less reliable with small datasets
15.FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS▼

Can a Pareto chart track trends over time?
Only for one period. Use multiple charts for trend analysis.

What if two categories have the same frequency?
Use cost or severity as a secondary ranking.

Can we combine cost and frequency?
Yes, by assigning cost values to categories.

How are Pareto charts used in team reviews?
They highlight major sources of loss and guide discussion.

Can Pareto be automated?
Yes—most analytics tools support this.

Difference between vital few and critical few?
Vital few = based purely on data.
Critical few = vital few + items important for safety or compliance.
