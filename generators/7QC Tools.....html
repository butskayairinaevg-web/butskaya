INTRODUCTION

The 7 QC Tools, also called the Seven Basic Quality Tools, are simple graphical and statistical techniques used to find, analyze, and solve problems at work. These tools help teams understand issues in a process, improve product quality, and make better decisions. They are used across many industries because they are easy to learn and very effective for continuous improvement.

Common problem-solving methods where 7 QC tools are used

Kaizen

QC tools support small, continuous improvements by helping teams understand issues and track progress.

8D Problem-Solving

Pareto charts, check sheets, and control charts are used to identify major problems and verify solutions in the 8D methodology.

Quality Improvement Projects

QC tools help collect data, analyze issues, and monitor performance in structured quality improvement projects.

PDCA Cycle (Plan–Do–Check–Act)

Used for continuous improvement. QC tools help in the Plan and Check phases to analyze data and measure results.

DMAIC — Six Sigma

QC tools are used heavily in the Measure and Analyze phases to find the root cause and validate improvements.

Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

Tools like Fishbone Diagram and Pareto Chart help identify the most likely causes of a problem.

Kaizen & 8D

QC tools support Kaizen and 8D problem solving for small continuous improvements and structured corrective actions.

WHAT ARE 7 QC TOOLS?

The 7 Quality Tools are simple graphical and statistical techniques that are extremely effective for solving quality problems and improving processes. These tools are easy to understand and can be used without advanced analytical skills, making them practical for everyday use.

Quality Control (QC) and Quality Assurance (QA) engineers commonly apply these tools on a daily, weekly, or monthly basis to analyze product and process issues, prevent defects, and reduce non-value-added activities such as rework, repair, and rejection.

7 QC Tools List

  1. Cause-and-Effect Diagram (Fishbone/Ishikawa Diagram)
  2. Check Sheet
  3. Control Chart
  4. Histogram
  5. Pareto Chart
  6. Scatter Diagram
  7. Flowchart (Process Map)

1. Cause-and-Effect Diagram (Fishbone / Ishikawa Diagram)

A structured diagram used to identify all possible causes of a problem. It looks like a fishbone, where the “head” is the problem and the “bones” represent categories like Man, Machine, Material, Method, Measurement, and Environment.

Why it helps

This tool helps teams brainstorm and explore every possible reason behind a defect or performance issue.

2. Check Sheet

A simple form used to collect real-time data in a systematic way. It helps track how often something happens—like defects, delays, or errors. Because it is easy to use and requires no calculations, it is ideal for daily shop-floor data collection and spotting patterns.

3. Control Chart

A graphical tool used to monitor how a process changes over time. It has a center line (average) and two control limits (upper and lower), which show the expected variation. If points fall outside limits or show unusual patterns, it signals that the process may be out of control and requires correction.

4. Histogram

A bar graph that shows how data is distributed across different ranges. It visually represents the frequency of values, helping understand variations, patterns, or abnormalities in the process. It is useful for identifying whether the process output is consistent or spread too widely.

5. Pareto Chart

A special type of bar graph based on the 80/20 rule—80% of problems come from 20% of causes. It helps identify the most important issues to focus on first by arranging problems from biggest to smallest. This enables teams to prioritize efforts for maximum impact.

6. Scatter Diagram

A graph that shows the relationship between two variables. If the points form a clear pattern (upward or downward), it indicates that one variable may be influencing the other. This is especially helpful for verifying cause-and-effect assumptions.

7. Flowchart (Process Map)

A step-by-step diagram showing how a process works from start to finish. It uses symbols like rectangles, diamonds, and arrows to show activities and decisions. Flowcharts help identify bottlenecks, redundancies, and improvement opportunities in workflows.

The 7 QC Tools help organizations identify, analyze, control, and eliminate process and product-related problems effectively.

Why Do We Need 7 QC Tools?

The 7 QC Tools are essential because they help organizations identify, analyse, control, and eliminate problems in products and processes. They are simple, powerful, and universally applicable.

Identify the Real Problem

Most companies fail because they assume the problem instead of verifying it with data. 7 QC Tools convert opinions into facts: Check Sheet → Real data, Pareto → Which problem is biggest, Fishbone → Actual root cause.

Reduce Defects & Improve Quality

These tools help monitor defect patterns, find the major contributors, and reduce recurring issues. Result: Fewer defects → Better product quality → Higher customer satisfaction.

Reduce Process Variation

Control Charts detect abnormal variation, Scatter Diagrams show relationships, Histograms show spread of data. Outcome: Stable and predictable processes.

NEW 7 QC Tools (7 Management & Planning Tools)

These are advanced, management-oriented tools used for strategic planning, problem solving, decision making, goal setting, and organizing complex information. They focus on ideas, relationships, decisions, and planning rather than basic data analysis.

  1. Affinity Diagram (KJ Method)
  2. Tree Diagram
  3. Relation Diagram (Interrelationship Diagram)
  4. Matrix Diagram
  5. Matrix Data Analysis
  6. PDPC – Process Decision Program Chart
  7. Arrow Diagram (PERT/CPM)

Details: New 7 QC Tools

Affinity Diagram (KJ Method)

Purpose: Organize large amounts of ideas, opinions, or issues into related groups. Used when the situation is chaotic or the team has too many ideas. Benefit: Helps pinpoint the problem and cluster ideas logically.

Tree Diagram

Purpose: Break down a broad goal into detailed sub-goals, tasks, or strategies. Benefit: Clear alignment and agreement within the team.

Relation Diagram

Purpose: Identify cause-and-effect relationships among complex, interrelated issues. Benefit: Shows which issues are drivers (causes) and which are effects.

Matrix Diagram & Matrix Data Analysis

Purpose: Show relationships between two or more categories of information and, where needed, apply numerical analysis for quantitative decision making. Applications: Process analysis, market surveys, new product planning, design reviews.

PDPC — Process Decision Program Chart

Purpose: Identify risks and countermeasures in a plan. Used for contingency planning and ensuring a plan succeeds by preparing for problems in advance.

Arrow Diagram (PERT/CPM)

Purpose: Scheduling and sequencing tasks in a project. Shows dependencies, critical path and timeline to help optimise schedule control.
