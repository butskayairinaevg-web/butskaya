Process Flow Chart in Manufacturing

(Symbols, Types, Applications, and Best Practices)

1. Meaning and Definition

A Process Flow Chart (PFC) is a graphical representation of the sequence of operations, inspections, decisions, and movements involved in a manufacturing or service process. It presents each step in logical and chronological order, showing how inputs are transformed into outputs.

A Process Flow Chart is also commonly known as:

  • Process Flow Diagram (PFD)
  • Process Map
  • Workflow Diagram

In manufacturing and quality management, the PFC serves as a foundational analysis tool that enables teams to understand the actual process, not just the documented one.

2. Role of Process Flow Chart in Quality and Manufacturing

In manufacturing and quality improvement, clarity is critical.

Before solving any problem or improving a process, the team must first see and agree on how the process truly works.

A Process Flow Chart:

  • Visually represents how work flows
  • Makes complex processes easy to understand
  • Acts as a common language between operators, engineers, managers, and auditors

For quality professionals, a flow chart is often the starting point before applying:

  • Cause & Effect (Fishbone) Diagram
  • Check Sheet
  • Pareto Chart
  • Control Chart

Without a clear flow chart, problem-solving efforts are often based on assumptions rather than facts.

3. Where and Why Process Flow Charts Are Used

  • Manufacturing processes
  • Project planning
  • Service industries
  • Supply chain and logistics
  • Quality problem-solving methodologies

They are mandatory supporting documents during:

  • New product development
  • Process validation
  • Quality audits
  • APQP and PPAP submissions

4. When to Use a Process Flow Chart

  • Defining or analysing a new process
  • Standardising an existing process
  • Redesigning or improving a process
  • Identifying bottlenecks, rework loops, delays, or gaps
  • Preparing documentation for quality systems and audits

5. Types of Process Flow Charts

There are two main types of Process Flow Charts used in manufacturing.

5.1 High-Level Process Flow Chart

  • Typically 6–10 major steps
  • Shows only main phases or blocks
  • Does not go into operational details

Used when:

  • Starting a new project
  • Explaining the process to management
  • Identifying overall improvement priorities

5.2 Detailed Process Flow Chart

  • Usually 15 or more steps
  • Includes operations, inspections, decisions, delays, rework, and storage
  • Shows actual shop-floor reality

Used when:

  • Standardising work
  • Performing process improvement
  • Conducting PFMEA or root-cause analysis
  • Preparing audit-ready documentation

6. Symbols Used in Process Flow Charts

Standard symbols ensure universal understanding and consistency.

Flow chart SymbolsMeaning and use cases

RectangleOperation

Document SymbolDocuments used or updated

Using standard symbols avoids confusion during cross-functional reviews and audits.

7. Key Elements of a Process Flow Chart

  • Start or End
  • Arrows / Flow-lines
  • Process / Operation
  • Decision
  • Storage
  • Delay / Wait
  • Document

8. Purpose of a Process Flow Chart in Manufacturing

  • Visualise the workflow from raw material to finished product
  • Identify bottlenecks, waste, and inefficiencies
  • Standardise quality procedures and work instructions
  • Improve communication across departments
  • Support training of new employees
  • Form the basis for process risk analysis

9. Process Flow Diagram – Key Quality Requirements

  • Reflect complete process from receiving to shipping
  • Identify outsourced operations
  • Identify stations where special characteristics are verified
  • Include sequence numbers and inspection stations
  • Include unique process equipment
  • Include rework and reinspection
  • Include packaging, labeling and storage

These points are critical for PPAP approval and audit compliance.

10. Importance of Flow Charts in Quality Improvement

  • Identify redundant steps
  • Detect inefficient sequences
  • Understand sources of variation and defects
  • Provide foundation for other QC tools

11. Step-by-Step: How to Create a Process Flow Chart

Step 1: Define the Process

Clearly define:

  • Process name
  • Start point
  • End point

Step 2: Identify All Steps

List every:

  • Operation
  • Inspection
  • Decision
  • Delay or storage

Step 3: Arrange Steps Sequentially

Place the steps in the correct order from start to finish. Make sure arrows show the logical flow of the process

Step 4: Select Standard Symbols

Use standard shapes (rectangles, diamonds, ovals, etc.) so that everyone understands the chart easily

Step 5: Connect with Arrows

Ensure arrows clearly show:

  • Forward flow
  • Rework loops
  • Alternate paths

Step 6: Review with the Team

Validate the chart with the process owner or cross-functional team to confirm accuracy and completeness

Step 7: Use it for Analysis

Now that you have a clear visual, analyze it to find non-value-added steps, duplication, or possible failure points.

12. Real-World Practical Example

Automotive Door Welding Process

Start → Load Door Panel → Welding → Visual Inspection → Leak Test → Decision (OK?) → Yes → Store for Next Process → End No → Rework → Welding

13. Advantages and Limitations

Advantages

  • Clear visual communication
  • Identifies waste and bottlenecks
  • Improves cross-functional understanding
  • Supports standardisation and audits

Limitations

  • Can become complex for large systems
  • Requires regular updates
  • Does not measure performance numerically

14. Best Practices

  • Create the chart with the process team
  • Keep flow left-to-right or top-to-bottom
  • Use consistent symbols
  • Clearly define start and end points
  • Update whenever the process changes

15. Common Mistakes and Prevention

  • Skipping steps – Observe the process physically
  • Inconsistent symbols – Follow standard symbols
  • Ignoring operators – Always involve shop-floor staff
  • Missing arrows – Clearly show direction
  • Overcomplication – Keep it simple and readable

16. Flow Chart Checklist

  • ✔ Process boundaries defined
  • ✔ All steps listed sequentially
  • ✔ Correct symbols used
  • ✔ Flow arrows connected
  • ✔ Reviewed and approved

17. Summary / Key Takeaways

A Process Flow Chart is one of the most powerful and fundamental quality tools. It transforms complex manufacturing processes into clear, visual, and actionable information. Before performing any problem-solving, improvement, or risk analysis activity, start with a flow chart — it is your roadmap to process clarity.
