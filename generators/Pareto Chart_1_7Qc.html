1. Introduction ▼

Pareto Charts are widely used in manufacturing and quality management to analyze defect data and identify which issues occur most frequently. They provide a simple visual representation of problem categories, their frequency, and their cumulative contribution to the total. This helps teams on the shop floor quickly understand which issues should be addressed first for maximum impact.

A Pareto Chart combines a bar graph and a line graph to display both the magnitude of defects and their cumulative effect.

It is based on the Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule), which suggests that 80% of problems arise from 20% of causes.

By using Pareto Charts, teams can prioritise improvement activities, allocate resources effectively, and drive significant improvements in quality and performance.

2. What a Pareto Chart Shows ▼

A Pareto Chart includes two key components:

2.1 Bars (Vertical Bar Graph)

Bars represent defect categories arranged from highest to lowest frequency.

They show:

  • The count/frequency/cost of each problem
  • Which defect is the largest contributor
  • Comparison between various defect categories
  • The "vital few" issues that need immediate attention

Example:

DefectCount
Scratches45
Colour Variation25
Wrong Dimensions15

Bars will appear with heights 45 → 25 → 15.

2.2 Line (Cumulative Percentage Line)

The line overlays the bars and shows how each category accumulates toward 100% of total defects.

It helps teams understand:

  • How quickly problems accumulate
  • Where the 80% impact zone lies
  • Which defects have minimal impact

Example:

Total defects = 85

DefectCountCumulative %
Scratches4553%
Colour Variation2582%
Wrong Dimensions15100%

The line rises steeply at first, then flattens.

3. Understanding Percentages and Cumulative Percentages ▼

The line in a Pareto Chart represents the cumulative percentage of defects.

To understand this clearly, look at the data table below:

Example Data Table

Type of Defect Frequency % of Total Cumulative %

Total59––

How % of Total Is Calculated

For Collar Defects:

(10 / 59) × 100 = 16.9%

How Cumulative % Works

Cumulative percentage is the running total of all percentage values from top to bottom.

For Collar Defects:

39.0% (Button) + 27.1% (Pocket) + 16.9% (Collar) = 83.1%

Important Points

  • The final cumulative percentage will always be 100%.
  • Cumulative percentages help you understand what proportion of all defects can be eliminated if you solve the top issues.

Example Interpretation

In this example:

  • The top two defects (Button + Pocket) = 66.1% of all defects.
  • This means solving just these two problems will eliminate 66% of total defects, giving a major improvement with minimal effort.
  • Solving two issues leads to major improvement.
  • Cumulative % always ends near 100%.

Reading the Cumulative Line

  • It is the running total of percentages.
  • When the line is steep, the defects have a strong impact. These should be prioritized. (Steep rise = high-impact defects.)
  • When the line flattens, the remaining defects have less effect. Solving them will not produce major improvement. (Flat region = small contributors.)
4. How Bars and Line Work Together ▼

Bars provide absolute values, while the cumulative line provides relative importance.

Together, they answer critical questions:

  • Which defect should be addressed first?
  • Which few issues create most of the defects?
  • How much improvement can be achieved by fixing top issues?
  • When does solving more issues stop yielding significant returns?
5. The Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule) ▼

The rule states:

80% of problems come from 20% of causes.

In practice:

  • A few defect types cause most of the defects.
  • Addressing them generates the greatest improvement.
  • Even if the ratio isn't exact, the principle holds: Focus on the vital few, not the trivial many.

It helps with:

  • Prioritization
  • Resource allocation
  • Continuous improvement
6. Why Use a Pareto Chart? ▼

It helps teams answer:

  • What should we fix first?
  • Which defects are most costly or frequent?
  • Where should we focus improvement efforts?
  • What is driving customer complaints or rework?

This is why Pareto Charts are one of the 7 QC Tools.

7. Common Uses of Pareto Charts ▼

Pareto Charts are widely used for:

  • Quality defect analysis
  • Customer complaint analysis
  • Machine breakdown causes
  • Supplier defect tracking
  • Safety incident analysis
  • Process waste or scrap analysis
8. How to Read a Pareto Chart ▼
  1. Look at the tallest bars → biggest contributors.
  2. Follow the cumulative line → shows impact accumulation.
  3. Identify the 70–80% zone → these issues drive the majority of problems.
  4. Observe where the line flattens → low-impact issues.
