What is Variation?

Variation and variability in quality control – including common & special causes, product level and process level variation, with examples and detailed explanations.

1. What is Variation?

Definition in simple words.

What is Variation?

Variation means the difference or deviation in a process, product, or service output from its expected or target value.

In simple words, no two products are exactly alike even if made under the same conditions.

2. Variability

Basic understanding of variability.

Variability

  • Meaning: No two things are exactly the same.
  • Nature itself shows variability → size, colour, weight, shape, performance, etc.
  • In manufacturing: Every product made will have small differences, even under the same conditions.
  • Example: If we make 100 bolts of 10 mm, not all will be exactly 10.000 mm. Some may be 9.98, some 10.02 mm.

3. 🏭 Why Variation Occurs?

Main sources (5M + E).

🏭 Why Variation Occurs?

Because every process is affected by several factors such as:

  • Material properties
  • Machine condition
  • Man
  • Method
  • Environment

4. 🌳 Types of Variation ( Product Lavel ) – Overview

Product level variation – detailed in next sections.

🌳 Types of Variation ( Product Lavel)

Below are the main types of variation at product level. Each type is explained in separate sections.

5. Common Cause Variation (Product Level)

Inherent / natural variation.

1. Common Cause Variation

Also called: Inherent / Natural Variation

These are natural variations that are always present in the process.

They come from normal and routine causes.

The process is stable but not perfect.

Examples:

  • Small temperature fluctuation in the workshop
  • Slight difference in raw material hardness
  • Minor difference in measurement by same operator

6. Special Cause Variation (Product Level)

Assignable cause variation.

2 Special Cause Variation

Also called: Assignable Cause Variation

These variations occur due to specific or identifiable reasons.

They are not part of the normal process and must be found and eliminated.

Examples:

  • Wrong machine setting
  • Tool wear or breakage
  • Power failure
  • Wrong raw material batch

7. Within-piece Variation

Differences inside a single product.

3. Within-piece Variation

Differences found within a single product.

Example:
The thickness of a metal plate is 5 mm at one end and 5.2 mm at the other end.

8. Piece-to-piece Variation

Differences between products.

4 Piece-to-piece Variation

Differences between different products made one after another under the same conditions.

Example:
First shaft diameter = 20.00 mm, next = 20.05 mm.

9. Time-to-time Variation

Shift-wise / time-wise differences.

5.Time-to-time Variation

Differences observed at different times (e.g., shift to shift, morning vs evening).

Example:
Morning shift produces smooth surface, night shift rough surface.

⚙️ Practical Example

12. 🌳 Types of Variation ( Process Lavel )

Process level variation – three main types.

🌳 Types of Variation ( Process Lavel)

There are three types of process variation present in the manufacturing process:

  • 1. Inherent (Common Cause) Process Variation
    → Normal, unavoidable fluctuation in the process.
  • 2. Assignable (Special Cause) Process Variation
    → Specific, identifiable abnormal reason causing instability.
  • 3. Total Process Variation
    → Combined effect of inherent + assignable variations.

13. Inherent (Common Cause) Process Variation

Also called common cause variation.

1. Inherent (Common Cause) Process Variation

• Definition: it is also called Common Cause Variation. This is the natural or unavoidable variability that exists in any manufacturing or service process — even when the process is operating correctly and consistently.

🔹 Types of Variation:

1. Common Cause Variation

o These are the natural fluctuations that are always present in a stable process.

o Example: Minor temperature changes, machine vibration, slight differences in raw material properties.

Common cause (Chance causes) :

  • • Common causes are always present on the process.
  • • Sources of variations present in process is stable over the time.
  • • Process is said to be under statistical control.
  • • Voice of process is predictable.
  • • Output of process is predictable.
  • • Local action required to control process variation.

A control chart having a Common cause will have the following characteristics:

  • • Very few points will be near to the control limits.
  • • Most of the points will lie near to the central line.

14. Assignable (Special Cause) Process Variation

Specific identifiable abnormal reasons.

2. Assignable (Special Cause) Process Variation Definition:

Unexpected changes in a process that happen because of unusual or outside factors, which are not normally part of the process. These occur due to specific identifiable reasons, not normally part of the process.

• Example: Tool breakage, operator error, incorrect material batch.

👉 Example: Machine breakdown, wrong material used, or operator mistake.

  • • Special causes do not always happen in the process.
  • • When they occur, the process is not under statistical control.
  • • the process output becomes unpredictable.
  • • the process is not stable over time.
  • • Sudden big changes (erratic fluctuations or shifts) can be seen in the process.
  • • Management action is required to find and remove the cause.

15. Types of Special Causes (Control Chart Patterns)

Shift, trend, erratic, cyclic patterns.

Types of Special Causes

  • 1. Shift
    o When several consecutive points on a control chart move above or below the central line, indicating a sudden change in the process.
    o Cause: Machine setting change, raw material change, environment change.
  • 2. Trend (Indication of Trend)
    o When points gradually increase or decrease over time, showing a slow shift in the process.
    o Cause: Tool wear, gradual temperature change, operator habit.
  • 3. Erratic Fluctuations
    o Random, unpredictable spikes or drops in process output.
    o Cause: Power failure, material defect, human error, machine malfunction.
  • 4. Cyclic Patterns (Extreme Variations)
    o Repeating patterns at regular intervals in the process output.
    o Cause: Seasonal changes, machine vibration, batch process timing.

16. Extreme Variations

Large, abnormal changes beyond normal range.

Extreme Variations:

Extreme variations are large, abnormal changes or deviations in a process or measurement that go far beyond the normal or expected range.

Explanation:

In a controlled process, variation should remain within upper and lower control limits. When data points go outside these limits or show very high peaks and low dips, it indicates extreme variation — usually caused by serious process issues or external factors.

Example:

If the normal diameter of a shaft is 10.00 ± 0.05 mm, and suddenly readings like 9.70 mm or 10.30 mm appear, these are extreme variations.

Possible Causes:

  • 1. Tool breakage or wear
  • 2. Machine malfunction
  • 3. Wrong raw material or setup
  • 4. Operator mistake
  • 5. Environmental change (temperature, pressure, humidity, etc.)

Effects:

  • • Product goes out of specification
  • • Increased rejection and rework
  • • Process instability
  • • Quality control failure

Solutions:

  • • Immediately stop the process and investigate the root cause
  • • Check machine and tool condition
  • • Verify raw material and setup parameters
  • • Implement corrective and preventive actions (CAPA)

17. Erratic Fluctuations

Irregular, unpredictable changes.

Erratic Fluctuations

🔹 Meaning (अर्थ):

Erratic fluctuations refer to irregular, unpredictable, and inconsistent changes in a process, measurement, or data pattern.

🔹 In Quality Control :

In SPC (Statistical Process Control), erratic fluctuations indicate that the process is not stable and may be affected by special causes (like machine malfunction, wrong material, operator error, etc.).

🔹 Example :

Suppose during inspection, the diameter readings of a shaft are:

10.00, 10.02, 10.05, 9.85, 10.20, 9.90

→ These values show erratic fluctuations because the data jumps up and down irregularly.

🔹 Causes:

  • 1. Machine vibration or instability
  • 2. Operator inconsistency
  • 3. Raw material variation
  • 4. Temperature or environmental change
  • 5. Improper tool or fixture condition

🔹 Effect:

  • • Poor product quality
  • • Process out of control
  • • High rejection or rework rate

18. Shifts

Sudden movement of process mean to new level.

Shifts

Definition:

A shift occurs when the entire process output suddenly moves from one average level to another, while still remaining stable around the new level. It indicates a sudden and permanent change in the process mean (average).

Explanation:

In a control chart, a shift appears as a group of consecutive points all above or below the central line (mean). This means something in the process has changed — such as a machine setting, material batch, or measurement system.

Example:

If the average diameter of a part was consistently around 10.00 mm, and then suddenly all values start to center around 10.10 mm, this is a shift in the process.

Possible Causes:

  • 1. Change in machine setting or calibration
  • 2. New operator or method introduced
  • 3. Change in raw material batch or supplier
  • 4. Environmental change (temperature, humidity, etc.)
  • 5. Tool replacement or wear adjustment

Effects:

  • • Process mean moves away from target
  • • Possible product nonconformance
  • • Reduced process capability
  • • Incorrect conclusions if shift is unnoticed

Solutions:

  • • Identify and correct the reason for the shift
  • • Review calibration and setup parameters
  • • Ensure process standardization
  • • Monitor the control chart regularly

19. Trends

Gradual continuous movement in one direction.

Trends

Definition:

A trend is a gradual, continuous movement of process data in one direction — either upward or downward — over time. It shows that the process is slowly changing rather than remaining stable.

Explanation:

In a control chart, a trend appears as a series of consecutive points that consistently increase or decrease. This indicates a progressive drift in the process, often due to wear, fatigue, or environmental influence.

Example:

If the measured diameter of a part slowly increases from 10.00 → 10.02 → 10.04 → 10.06 → 10.08 mm, it shows an upward trend — possibly due to tool wear or thermal expansion.

Possible Causes:

  • 1. Tool wear or machine aging
  • 2. Gradual change in temperature or pressure
  • 3. Material property changes over time
  • 4. Progressive operator fatigue
  • 5. Improper maintenance or lubrication schedule

Effects:

  • • Process drifts out of control limits
  • • Product dimensions gradually move out of tolerance
  • • Increased scrap or rework over time
  • • Reduced process reliability

Solutions:

  • • Regular tool and machine maintenance
  • • Continuous monitoring using control charts
  • • Periodic recalibration of instruments
  • • Implement preventive measures to stop drift early
