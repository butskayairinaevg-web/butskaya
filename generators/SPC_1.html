SPC FOUNDATION

1. Introduction

Statistical Process Control (SPC) is a systematic method of monitoring, controlling, and improving manufacturing processes using statistical techniques.

Developed in 1924 by William A. Shewhart at Bell Laboratories, SPC introduced control charts to identify process variability. SPC gained widespread adoption during World War II and was later embraced by Japanese and global manufacturing industries. Today, it is a core tool in quality management.

2. What is SPC?

SPC is a method for measuring and controlling quality by monitoring the manufacturing process in real time. Data is collected from product dimensions, features, or process instrumentation readings and plotted on control charts with predetermined limits. By analyzing this data, processes are maintained at optimal performance, enabling prevention of defects and continuous improvement.

SPC Principles

  • Every product and process is unique; no two items are exactly alike.
  • Manufacturing processes are dynamic and subject to continuous change.
  • Variation is inherent in all processes and is a natural occurrence.
  • All variations, whether in products or processes, can be measured and analyzed to improve quality.

3. Why Use SPC?

SPC enables manufacturers to:

  • Shift from inspection-based to prevention-based quality control
  • Detect trends, shifts, or deviations in real time
  • Reduce scrap, rework, and defects
  • Support continuous improvement, cost reduction, and process optimization
  • Ensure consistent product quality and customer satisfaction

4. Objectives of SPC

  • Collecting the data and drawing meaningful results.
  • Finding out the root cause of the problem.
  • Understanding and reducing process variations.
  • Predicting future tendencies.
  • Preventing defects to improve product and process quality.
  • Continuous process improvement.
  • Improve Customer satisfaction.
SPC TOOLS & STRATEGY

SPC Tools

The important popular basic tools are:

  • Control Charts
  • Run Chart
  • Histogram or Frequency Distribution Diagram
  • Process Capability Analysis tools

SPC Strategy

The first strategy is to bring the process under control by removing special causes present in the manufacturing process.

The second strategy is to improve process capability by reducing common causes or chance causes present in the manufacturing process.

Benefits of SPC

  • Early detection of deviations
  • Reduction in scrap, rework, and waste
  • Improved process stability and quality
  • Supports data-driven decision making
  • Enables continuous improvement
  • Ensures compliance with quality standards and customer requirements
CONTROL CHART CREATION

Steps for Creating a Control Chart

  1. Select quality characteristics that need to be controlled.
  2. Decide the subgroup size i.e. number of units to be taken in a sample.
  3. Decide the subgroup frequency.
  4. Collect data on the data collection check sheet.
  5. Fill up the control chart data sheet.
  6. Calculate the X-bar and Range R for each subgroup.
  7. Calculate the grand average X-bar and average range R-bar.
  8. Calculate Trial Control Limits.
  9. Process Capability Study.
  10. Actions for Improvement.
CONTROL CHART TYPES & BASICS

Control Charts – Types

SPC Control Charts are classified into two main categories:

  • Variable-type Control Charts: length, weight, diameter, temperature
  • Attribute type Control Charts: OK/Not OK, Pass/Fail, number of defects

Control Chart Selection

  • Variable: Individual – Moving Range, X-bar – R, X-bar – S
  • Attribute: p-chart, u-chart
Control chart selection depends on the type of data (variable vs. attribute) and the subgrouping approach used in the process.

📊 What Is a Control Chart?

A Control Chart is a graphical tool used to monitor the performance of a process over time and detect any unusual variations.

🧩 Key Components

Component Description
Central Line (CL) Represents the process average or mean.
Upper Control Limit (UCL) The upper boundary of expected variation.
Lower Control Limit (LCL) The lower boundary of expected variation.
Data Points Actual measurements plotted over time to track process behavior.

🛠️ Purpose

  • Detect process shifts, trends, or extreme variations
  • Differentiate between common cause and special cause variation
  • Support decision-making for process adjustments
INTERPRETATION OF CONTROL CHART

Extreme Variations – Out of Control

Extreme variation is recognized by the points falling outside the Upper and Lower control limits.

Causes of Extreme Variations (Out-of-Control Points)

Extreme variations, indicated by single points falling outside the Upper Control Limit (UCL) or Lower Control Limit (LCL), are signs of an assignable cause impacting the process. Common causes include:

  • Measurement and Data Errors: Mistakes made during data collection, such as errors in measurements or calculations, or data that is miscalculated or misplotted.
  • Improper Setup: Issues with the process setup, like the wrong setting of the machine, tools, or equipment.
  • Non-Representative Sampling: Selecting samples during non-typical periods, for instance, when samples were chosen only at the start or the end of an operation before the process stabilized or after it began to degrade.

⚙ Process Shift in Control Charts

A Process Shift is an out-of-control condition that signals a sustained change in the process average, indicating that a new, persistent factor is influencing the process.

  • Definition: A shift is detected when a series of consecutive points falls entirely on one side (either above or below) the center line (Xˉ or average) on the control chart.
  • Detection Rule (The "Run of 7"): It is generally assumed that a significant shift has occurred when seven or more consecutive points lie above or below the center line.
  • Seven points above the centre line → Shift towards the upper side.
  • Seven points below the centre line → Shift towards the lower side.

🔧 Common Assignable Causes of a Process Shift

The following factors often introduce a systematic change that moves the process average, leading to a shift pattern on the control chart:

  • Equipment and Setup Changes:
    • Change in Machine Setting (e.g., a setting drifting or being incorrectly adjusted).
    • Loose Fixture, Tooling, or Wear (e.g., a clamp coming loose, or tool wear gradually changing the dimension).
  • Material Changes:
    • Change in Material (e.g., a new supplier, a different batch with varying properties, or a change in raw material quality).
  • Personnel and Measurement Factors:
    • Change in Operator, Inspector, or Inspection Equipment (e.g., a newly trained operator, a different measurement device used, or equipment that has gone out of calibration).
    • New or Unskilled Operator or a general carelessness of the operator (introducing consistent bias or different work methods).

Trend in Control Chart

A trend is indicated when a series of consecutive points on the control chart move continuously upward or downward. This shows that the process is gradually drifting in one direction instead of remaining stable.

  • Generally, if 7 consecutive points keep increasing (upward) or decreasing (downward), it is considered a trend.
  • A trend is a warning signal that the process may go out of control if corrective actions are not taken.

Examples of Trend:

  • Points rising continuously → Process mean is increasing.
  • Points falling continuously → Process mean is decreasing.

Possible Causes of Trend:

  • Gradual wear and tear of tools or machines.
  • Slow deterioration of measuring instruments.
  • Continuous change in material properties (e.g., hardness, thickness).
  • Environmental factors like temperature or humidity changes.
  • Operator’s changing method or increasing fatigue.

Erratic Fluctuations in Control Chart

Erratic fluctuations are sudden, irregular, and unpredictable variations in the plotted points of a control chart. Unlike a shift or a trend, which follow a pattern, erratic fluctuations do not show any consistent direction. Instead, the points move up and down randomly and sharply.

Indication:

  • Points fluctuate in a zig-zag manner, without forming a steady pattern.
  • Control chart looks unstable and scattered.
  • May cross control limits frequently or stay very close to them.

Possible Causes of Erratic Fluctuations:

  • Variation in raw material quality.
  • Operator inconsistency or carelessness.
  • Faulty or unstable measuring instruments.
  • Machine vibration, looseness, or poor maintenance.
  • Uncontrolled environmental conditions (e.g., sudden temperature or pressure changes).
  • Poor or unstable process settings.

Conclusion:

Erratic fluctuations are a sign of special causes of variation. The process is not stable, and immediate investigation is required to identify and eliminate the root cause.
