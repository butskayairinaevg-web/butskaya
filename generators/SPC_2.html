PROCESS CAPABILITY • THEORY

Process Capability Indices: Cp and Cpk

A Process Capability Index tells us how well a process is performing with respect to the specification limits set by the customer. It checks whether the process can consistently produce products within tolerance limits.

Cp – Process Potential Cpk – Actual Capability Histograms & Control Charts Six Sigma – Variation Reduction

1. Process Capability – Overview

What is a Process Capability Index?

A Process Capability Index tells us how well a process is performing with respect to the specification limits (LSL & USL) set by the customer. It checks whether the process can consistently produce products within tolerance limits.

In process capability analysis:

  • Histogram and Control Charts are two of the basic 7 QC Tools used in process capability analysis.
  • Six Sigma Methodology is used to improve process capability by reducing process variation.
✅
Key idea: Capability is not only about the average value; it is about the entire distribution of data staying within spec limits over time.

2. Process Potential Index (Cp)

Cp measures potential capability – spread only.

Cp measures the potential capability of the process. It compares the total specification width to the natural spread of the process (6σ), assuming the process mean (μ) is perfectly centered between the specification limits. Cp only accounts for process variation.

Formula Cp = (USL − LSL) / 6σ
USL
Upper Specification Limit
LSL
Lower Specification Limit
σ (Sigma)
Standard Deviation – process spread
Cp value Interpretation Focus
< 1.0 Process variation is larger than specifications – Not capable. Reduce variation
= 1.0 Process just meets customer requirement / specifications. Good potential, but tight
> 1.0 Process variation is smaller than specifications – Capable. Good potential
≥ 1.33 Generally accepted minimum for a highly capable process. Excellent potential
Higher Cp Better potential capability. More robust process
ℹ️
Remember: Cp assumes the process mean is perfectly centered. A high Cp alone can be misleading if the mean has shifted toward one spec limit.

3. Process Performance Index (Cpk)

Cpk measures actual capability – spread + centering.

Cpk measures the actual capability of the process by considering both:

  • Process variation (spread, σ)
  • Shift of the process mean (μ) from the target

Cpk is the minimum of the capability for the upper and lower limits. It reflects the side where the process is closest to the specification limit.

Formula Cpk = min [ (USL − μ) / 3σ , (μ − LSL) / 3σ ]

Here μ (Mu) is the process mean. The minimum value indicates the σ-distance from the mean to the nearest specification limit.

Condition Implication Required action
Cpk < 1.0 Process is not capable; will produce defects. Improve centering and/or reduce variation
Cpk ≥ 1.33 Process is highly capable and well-centered. Maintain process control
Cpk = Cp Process is capable and effectively centered between LSL & USL. Focus on maintaining control
Cpk < Cp Process mean is off-center. Shift the process mean (μ) to the centre
Cpk ≥ 2.0 World-class capability. Benchmark / best-in-class process
📌
Cpk – Process Capability Index: It is a statistical index that measures the actual capability of a process. It considers both process variation (spread) and process centering (mean shift) with respect to specification limits.
  • Cpk < 1.0 → Process not capable (produces defects).
  • Cpk = 1.0 → Process is just capable (meets limits).
  • Cpk > 1.0 → Process is capable (good quality).
  • Cpk ≥ 1.33 → Generally accepted as industry standard.
  • Cpk ≥ 2.0 → World-class capability.
💡
  • Cp measures only spread (variation).
  • Cpk measures spread + centering (how close mean is to target).
  • Always Cpk ≤ Cp – if the mean is off-center, Cpk decreases.

4. Interpretation of Cp and Cpk

Capable vs not capable – what the numbers mean.

4.1 Cp (Process Capability)

  • Cp > 1:
    • Process spread is small.
    • All products fall within the specification limits.
    • Process is quite capable of meeting specifications.
  • Cp = 1:
    • Process spread exactly fits within specification limits.
    • Process is just capable of meeting specifications.
  • Cp < 1:
    • Process spread is large.
    • Many products fall outside specification limits.
    • Process is incapable of meeting specifications.

4.2 Cpk (Process Capability Index)

  • Cp = Cpk:
    • Process mean is exactly at the center – well-centered process.
  • Cpk < 1:
    • Process mean is shifted from the target.
    • Defects will be produced.
    • Process is not capable.
  • Cpk > 1:
    • Process mean may be slightly shifted,
    • But the process is still capable of meeting design specifications.

5. Interpretation of Process Capability Histograms

Visual interpretation of Cp/Cpk and variation.

A process capability histogram shows the frequency distribution of measured data compared with specification limits (LSL & USL). It helps visualise whether the process is capable, centered and stable.

Common Cases

  • 1. Normal / Ideal Process
    • Histogram is centered at the mean / nominal.
    • Spread is small, leaving enough space to LSL and USL.
    • Even if the process drifts slightly, defects are unlikely.
    • ✅ Best condition.
  • 2. Shifted Process
    • Histogram is shifted toward LSL or USL.
    • A small additional shift can cause defects on one side.
    • Cpk will be lower than Cp (indicating off-center process).
  • 3. Large Process Spread
    • Histogram is too wide (variation is high).
    • Even if it is centered, many data points cross LSL/USL.
    • Cp < 1 → Process is not capable.
  • 4. Truncated Distribution
    • Histogram appears “cut-off” at one or both ends.
    • Indicates defective parts may have been removed (manual inspection / segregation).
    • Can give a false impression of process capability.

Cp and Cpk in Histograms:

  • Cp compares the process spread (6σ) with the specification width.
  • Cpk considers both spread and centering.
  • Cp = Cpk → Process is well-centered.
  • Cpk < Cp → Process is shifted → more risk of defects.
🔎
Key Point: Process capability histograms + Cp/Cpk values together give a complete picture:
  • Cp ≥ 1.33 and Cpk ≈ Cp → Good, capable and centered process.
  • Cp < 1 or Cpk ≪ Cp → Process needs improvement (reduce variation or re-center).

6. Cp and Cpk Difference + Pp

Potential vs actual capability, and NPD performance.

Cp and Cpk both measure process capability, but they focus on different aspects. Pp is used during New Product Development (NPD).

Aspect Cp (Process Capability) Cpk (Process Capability Index)
Meaning Measures potential capability if the process is perfectly centered. Measures actual capability, considering how well the mean is centered.
Focus Only considers spread (variation) of the process. Considers both spread and centering of the process.
Assumption Assumes process mean is exactly in the middle of specification limits. No assumption — uses actual process mean.
Interpretation Shows process potential if it were perfectly centered. Shows real-world performance of the process.
When to Use Early stages – to see if variation is small enough for specs. During production – to check if process is actually within specs.
Key Limitation Can be misleading if mean is shifted. More realistic as it accounts for mean shift.

Cp Value – Normal Production Run vs NPD Stage

Cp value should be ≥ 1.33 during the normal production process.

The recommended value of Process Performance Capability – Pp is ≥ 1.67 during the New Product Development (NPD) stage.

Metric Stage Recommended value Meaning
Cp (Process Capability) Normal Production Run ≥ 1.33 Ensures the process is consistently producing within specification limits with enough margin for natural variation.
Pp (Process Performance Capability) New Product Development (NPD) Stage ≥ 1.67 Since data during NPD is limited and may include more variability, a higher value is recommended to ensure a robust process before mass production.
✅
Key Point:
  • Pp is used during NPD / prototype runs because it considers short-term performance with limited data.
  • Once the process stabilizes, Cp/Cpk are used for ongoing production capability.
  • A higher Pp (≥ 1.67) is expected in NPD to build confidence before ramping up.

7. Cp and Cpk – Process Capability Analysis (PCA)

Quick decision matrix for Cp & Cpk values.

This is a Process Capability Analysis (PCA) guide that evaluates a process based on Cp and Cpk values:

Cp Cpk Interpretation / Action
√ √ Process is capable. Continue production. Process is near the target.
√ X Process is potentially capable (variation is low), but mean is off-center.
Action: adjust process to bring Cpk closer to Cp / target.
X X Process is not capable. Management action required for improvement.

Key Points:

  1. Cp measures potential: low variation may exist, but doesn’t account for mean shift.
  2. Cpk measures actual performance: tells if the process is centred within spec limits.
  3. Ideal scenario: Cp ≈ Cpk and both ≥ 1.33 (or higher for critical products).
  4. Red flag: Cp ≥ 1.33 but Cpk < 1.33 → process spread is OK, but centering needs correction.

8. Conclusion

Cp for potential, Cpk for reality.

Cp and Cpk are crucial metrics in process capability analysis, each of which provides distinct perspectives on process performance.

  • Cp measures potential capability under ideal conditions (perfect centering).
  • Cpk offers a practical assessment by accounting for both spread and mean shift.

By understanding and using these metrics effectively, and combining them with histograms, control charts and SPC, you can improve process quality and overall performance.

🎯
Simple practical rule:
Aim for Cp ≥ 1.33 and Cpk ≈ Cp.
If Cp is good but Cpk is low → focus on centering.
If both Cp and Cpk are low → first reduce variation, then re-center.

9. SPC-3 – Special Causes of Variation (Preview)

Special causes • Assignable causes • Types of variations.

The next SPC topic is SPC-3 – Special Causes of Variation, also called Assignable causes. These are unusual variations that can be traced to specific reasons, unlike normal random variation.

Broadly, variations in a process are classified as:

  • Common causes – natural, random variation built into the process.
  • Special (assignable) causes – specific, identifiable sources of variation.

Understanding these causes is essential for correct use of control charts and effective process control.
